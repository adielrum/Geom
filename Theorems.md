**Theorem 1**
* **i.** Two lines of $P^{2}$ have exactly one point of intersection.
* **ii.** Two points of $P^{2}$ lie on exactly one line.

**Theorem 2**
Let P, Q, R, and S be four points of $P^{2}$, no three of which are collinear. Then there is a basis of $R^{3}$ with respect to which the four points have coordinates (1, 0, 0), (0, 1, 0), (0, 0, 1), and (1, 1, 1).

**Theorem 3**
Let $x$ and $y$ be homogeneous coordinate vectors of two points of $P^{2}$. Then $\lambda x+\mu y$ ($\lambda,\mu$ real) is a typical point on the line they determine.

**Theorem 4 (Desargues' theorem)**
Let PQR and $P^{\prime}Q^{\prime}R^{\prime}$ be triangles in $P^{2}$. Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{PP^{\prime}}$, $\overleftrightarrow{QQ^{\prime}}$, and $\overleftrightarrow{RR}^{\prime}$ are concurrent. Then $\overleftrightarrow{PQ}\cap\overleftrightarrow{P^{\prime}Q^{\prime}}$, $\overleftrightarrow{QR}\cap\overleftrightarrow{Q^{\prime}R^{\prime}}$, and $\overleftrightarrow{PR}\cap\overleftrightarrow{P^{\prime}R^{\prime}}$ are collinear.

**Theorem 5 (Pappus' theorem)**
Let $A_{1}B_{1}C_{1}$ and $A_{2}B_{2}C_{2}$ be collinear triples of points. Then the points $\overleftrightarrow{A_{1}B_{2}}\cap\overleftrightarrow{A_{2}B_{1}}=C_{3}$, $\overleftrightarrow{B_{2}C_{1}}\cap\overleftrightarrow{B_{1}C_{2}}=A_{3},$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_{1}C_{2}}\cap\overleftrightarrow{A_{2}C_{1}}=B_{3}$ are collinear.

**Theorem 6**
Let T: $E^{2}\rightarrow P^{2}$ be the mapping discussed in the text.
* **i.** Denote by $l_{\infty}$ the exceptional line of $P^{2}$. Then T maps $E^{2}$ bijectively to $P^{2}-l_{\infty}$.
* **ii.** Let P and Q be points of $E^{2}.$ Then TP and TQ determine a line $l^{\prime}$ of $P^{2},$ and T maps $l=\overleftrightarrow{PQ}$ bijectively to $l^{\prime}-l_{\infty}$.
* **iii.** Let $l$ and $m$ be lines of $E^{2}$. If $l\cap m=P$, then $l^{\prime}\cap m^{\prime}=TP.$ If $l||m$, then $l^{\prime}\cap m^{\prime}$ lies on $l_{\infty}$.

**Theorem 7**
Let PQR and P'Q'R' be triangles in $E^{2}$. Suppose that $\overleftrightarrow{PP}^{\prime}$, $\overleftrightarrow{QQ}^{\prime}$, and $\overleftrightarrow{RR}^{\prime}$ are parallel. Then:
* **i.** If $\overleftrightarrow{PQ}||\overleftrightarrow{P^{\prime}Q^{\prime}}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{QR}||\overleftrightarrow{Q^{\prime}R^{\prime}}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{PR}||\overleftrightarrow{P^{\prime}R^{\prime}}$ .
* **ii.** If $\overleftrightarrow{PQ}||\overleftrightarrow{P^{\prime}Q^{\prime}}$ but $\overleftrightarrow{QR}\cap\overleftrightarrow{Q^{\prime}R^{\prime}}=N,$ then $\overleftrightarrow{PR}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{P^{\prime}R^{\prime}}$ meet (say in M), and MN is parallel to $\overleftrightarrow{PQ}$ .
* **iii.** If $\overleftrightarrow{PQ}\cap\overleftrightarrow{P^{\prime}Q^{\prime}}=L$, $\overleftrightarrow{QR}\cap\overleftrightarrow{Q^{\prime}R^{\prime}}=M$ and $\overleftrightarrow{PR}\cap\overleftrightarrow{P^{\prime}R^{\prime}}=N$, then L, M, and N are collinear .

**Theorem 8**
Let PQR and $P^{\prime}Q^{\prime}R^{\prime}$ be triangles in $E^{2}$. Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{PP^{\prime}}$, $\overleftrightarrow{QQ}^{\prime}$, and $\overleftrightarrow{RR}^{\prime}$ meet in X. Then the conclusions of Theorem 7 hold.

**Theorem 9**
Let QRR'Q' be a trapezoid $(\overleftrightarrow{QQ}^{\prime}||\overleftrightarrow{RR}^{\prime})$. Let $l$ and $m$ be parallel lines through Q and R. Let $l^{\prime}$ and $m'$ be parallel lines through $Q^{\prime}$ and $R^{\prime}$. Let $X=l\cap l^{\prime}$, $Y=m\cap m^{\prime}$, and $Z=\overleftrightarrow{QR}\cap\overleftrightarrow{Q^{\prime}R^{\prime}}$ . Then X, Y, and Z are collinear.

**Theorem 10**
Let QRR'Q' be a parallelogram in $E^{2}$ ($\overleftrightarrow{QQ}^{\prime}||\overleftrightarrow{RR^{\prime}}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{Q^{\prime}R^{\prime}}||\overleftrightarrow{QR}$). Let $l$ and $m$ be parallel lines through Q and R. Let $l'$ and $m'$ be parallel lines through $Q^{\prime}$ and $R^{\prime}$ . If $X=l\cap l^{\prime}$ and $Y=m\cap m^{\prime}$ then $\overleftrightarrow{XY}$ is parallel to $\overleftrightarrow{QR}$.

**Theorem 11**
Let PQRS and $P^{\prime}Q^{\prime}R^{\prime}S^{\prime}$ be quadrangles. Then there is a unique $T\in PGL(2)$ such that $TP=P^{\prime}$, $TQ=Q^{\prime},$ $TR=R^{\prime}$, and $TS=S^{\prime}$.

**Theorem 12**
Every projective collineation has at least one fixed point and one fixed line.

**Theorem 13**
If P does not lie on $l$, the group of projective collineations leaving P and $l$ fixed is isomorphic to GL(2). Each such collineation can be uniquely written in the form $\begin{bmatrix}a&b&0\\ c&d&0\\ 0&0&1\end{bmatrix}$ where $ad-bc\ne0$ .

**Theorem 14**
Suppose that P lies on $l$. Then every projective collineation leaving P and $l$ fixed is uniquely represented by a matrix of the form $\begin{bmatrix}a&b&p\\ 0&c&q\\ 0&0&1\end{bmatrix}$ where $ac\ne0$ . Conversely, each such matrix determines a projective collineation leaving P and $l$ fixed.

**Theorem 15**
The transformation of Theorem 14 has exactly one fixed point and one fixed line if and only if $a=c=1$ and $bq\ne0$.

**Theorem 16**
A projective collineation with two fixed points may be written in the form $\begin{bmatrix}a&0&p\\ 0&c&q\\ 0&0&1\end{bmatrix}$ . Such a collineation has at least two fixed lines.

**Theorem 17**
If a projective collineation has three collinear fixed points, it may be written $\begin{bmatrix}a&0&0\\ 0&a&q\\ 0&0&1\end{bmatrix}$ with $a\ne0$ . Every point on the line $x_{1}=0$ is fixed. In addition, the line $x_{3}=0$ is a fixed line.

**Theorem 18**
When a perspective collineation is represented as in Theorem 17, it is:
* **i.** an elation if $a=1$ and $q\ne0$;
* **ii.** the identity if $a=1$ and $q=0$;
* **iii.** a homology if $a\ne1$.

**Theorem 19**
Suppose that a nontrivial perspective collineation with center P takes X to $X^{\prime}$. Then P, X, and $X^{\prime}$ are collinear.

**Theorem 20**
Let P be a point and $l$ a line. Let X and $X^{\prime}$ be points collinear with P. Assume that X and $X^{\prime}$ are not on $l$ and not equal to P. Then there is a unique perspective collineation with center P and axis $l$ that takes X to $X^{\prime}$ .

**Theorem 21**
Let P and Q be points of $P^{2}$ with respective polar lines $p$ and $q$. Then P lies on $q$ if and only if Q lies on $p$.

**Theorem 22**
Let P and Q be self-conjugate points of $P^{2}.$ Then $\overleftrightarrow{PQ}$ cannot be a self-conjugate line.

**Theorem 23**
A line contains exactly one self-conjugate point if and only if it is a self-conjugate line.

**Theorem 24**
Let $\pi u$ and $\pi v$ be points in $P^{2}.$ Then the line joining $\pi u$ and $\pi v$ has pole $\pi(u\times v)$.

**Theorem 25**
* **i.** Let $\{e_{1},e_{2},e_{3}\}$ be orthonormal with respect to $b$. Then, after replacing $e_{3}$ by its negative if necessary, we have $e_{1}\times e_{2}=b(e_{3},e_{3})e_{3}$, $e_{2}\times e_{3}=b(e_{1},e_{1})e_{1}$, and $e_{3}\times e_{1}=b(e_{2},e_{2})e_{2}$ .
* **ii.** For a given $b$ the number of occurrences of -1 among the $b(e_{i},e_{i})$ is independent of the choice of orthonormal basis.

**Theorem 26**
$(u\times v)\times w=(-1)^{s}(b(u,w)v-b(v,w)u),$ where the signature of $b$ is (r, s).
